Data Visualization

> background

My interest in data visualization rose from my work in social justice and gaming. When researching the role of online interactivity influencing offline action, I discovered Explorable Explanations, a digital movement focused on active learning. Explorable Explanations are interactive blog posts or visualizations that teach a concept by doing. Much of the work of a social activist is advocacy; simultaneously raising awareness and communicating the urgency of the issue. This involves designing for communication, interpreting highly technical datasets in a digestible way, and clearly displaying insights gleaned from the data. Visualizations blend my interests in information and play, and challenge both my design and development skills.

Below are samples of works I have completed or am currently pursuing. I'm always open to project ideas, so if you want to talk please shoot me a message!

> visualizing USA data

This is the second project for a data visualization course I took during spring 2016. It is a collection of univariate and bivariate color maps of various USA data, coded in d3.js. The hexagon representation of the country is an open-source contribution from the NPR Graphics team. My partner and I built functions to convert that data into a color spectrum. The project was an exercise in interpreting numerical data into accurate, readable graphs using changing color value. The project is hosted live on Github.

> sexual assault in game of thrones

While browsing online one day, I found an article about someone who had counted and compared all acts of rape in the popular TV series "Games of Thrones" with its print counterpart, the A Song of Ice and Fire series. (See the Tumblr post here.) One thing I noticed right away from the descriptions is that often, the person assaulted was an unnamed woman, and the assaulter was a heavy-hitting bad guy.

This reminded me of readings on how violence against women is used as a backdrop to further male character development - in this case, to showcase how evil a character really is. From the article, many of the women who were assaulted were minor characters, with a rape occasionally being their only participation in the entire series.

In order to bring more attention to this, I wanted to create a more complete data set archiving not just sexual assault in “Game of Thrones,” but all sex scenes in the series in order to make comparisons between on-screen sex, significant male characters, and discarded female characters. Right now I am putting together a .csv data set using episode descriptions from the Game of Throne Wikia. Once that is complete, I plan to visualize the data a) using network analysis to show which male characters commit the most assaults and whether they are represented as an antagonist in the series; b) interactivity in the network analysis to highlight unnamed female characters; and c) through bar charts to compare statistics on consensual and non-consensual sex scenes.

The image above is from a quick network analysis I wrote in R, which consists of the data provided in the analysis linked above. The primitive, undirected graph simply indicates that there are clusters of assaults all committed by a single, named male character. I look forward to fleshing out my ideas for visualizations in d3.js, especially including the new metadata I am adding from the data set I am building.
