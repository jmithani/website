background

I joined the staff of the South Side Weekly as a graphics contributor in September 2016. I specialize in providing infographics and data visualizations for both print and online editions. I've also volunteered to help writers obtain and analyze data for their stories.

a broken shield

"Cook County’s public defenders are overworked and underfunded. But criminal justice reform won’t succeed without them." – Eleanore Catolico, A Broken Shield

I made a series of graphics for the article, highlighting key facts in a way that could be easily comprehended. I rephrased and combined some of statistics reported - for instance, referring to the discrepency in defense time at one-third instead of only contrasting the two numbers.

I created a bar chart to easily communicate the extent to which Cook County public defenders were overextending themselves, highlighting the maximum case load they are supposed to be working with. I also pulled out statistics from the article (52% and 23%) and translated them into quantities of people (5 people versus 2 people) in order to show the human impact of a private versus public attorney.

school librarians, shelved

" CTU reported that in 2015, seventy-five percent of majority-African-American schools didn’t have a librarian, compared to sixteen percent of schools with a less than fifty percent African-American population. CPS’s own demographic analyses, conducted each year to determine quotas for selective enrollment schools, and defined using census data on socioeconomic status and other factors affecting student achievement, can be used to highlight the systemic disadvantages in library access." – Anne Li, School Librarians, Shelved

Final map that went to print in the South Side Weekly.

For this map, I used the open-source CPS Tiers Map by Open City Apps as well as public data on the locations of public schools in Chicago. I pulled all the data on high schools, then cross referenced whether each had a librarian based on a PDF report put out by the Chicago Teachers Union. I separated the data sets of with or without librarians into two different fusion tables, and finally I combined all three fusion tables into one interactive map. For the print version, I then screenshot the map and hand edited the icons and legend with Adobe Illustrator. To view the interactive, please visit the original article.

Shout out to the folks in the ChiHackNight #mapping Slack channel (several of who helped made the original Open City App!) for their guidance into the world of Google Fusion Tables.
